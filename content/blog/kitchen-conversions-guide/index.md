If you've ever tried to follow a recipe only to find it uses cups when you need grams, or millilitres when you have tablespoons, you know the frustration. Recipes from different countries use different measurement systems — and getting them wrong can ruin a dish.

This kitchen conversion guide covers every common cooking measurement conversion you'll need: volume, weight, temperature, and spoon sizes. Bookmark it, print it, or keep it open while you cook.

## The Two Measurement Systems in Cooking

| System | Used In | Volume Units | Weight Units |
|--------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Metric (grams, ml) | Europe, Asia, Australia, most of the world | Millilitres (ml), litres (L) | Grams (g), kilograms (kg) |
| Imperial / US Customary | US, UK (partially) | Cups, tablespoons, teaspoons, fluid ounces | Ounces (oz), pounds (lb) |

**Key difference:** In the US, a cup is 236.6 ml. In the UK (now mostly metric), an imperial cup is 284 ml. Stick to US standard for American recipes, metric for everything else.

### How to Measure Flour Correctly

The **spoon and level** method: fluff the flour with a spoon, spoon it into the measuring cup, then level off with a flat edge. Never scoop directly with the measuring cup — this packs the flour and can add 20-30% more.

### Butter by Country

| Country | Standard Pack Size | Markings |
|---------|-------------------|----------|
| US | 1 stick = 113g (4 oz) | Marked in tablespoons |
| UK / Europe | 250g block | Marked in 50g increments |
| Australia / NZ | 250g or 500g block | Marked in 50g increments |

A level tablespoon of butter weighs 14g. For cold butter, cut marks are surprisingly accurate — 1 tablespoon is about the size of your thumb tip.

## Liguid vs Dry Measuring Cups

**Important:** Use liquid measuring cups (glass with pour spout) for liquids and dry measuring cups (metal/plastic with flat top) for dry ingredients. They measure the same volume, but liquid cups let you read the meniscus at eye level, while dry cups let you level off the top with a straight edge.

## FAQ

**How many grams are in a cup of flour?**
125 grams for all-purpose flour. This varies by ingredient — a cup of sugar weighs 200g, a cup of butter weighs 227g.

**How many tablespoons in a cup?**
16 tablespoons in 1 cup (US). That's 4 tablespoons per quarter cup, 8 per half cup.

**How many ml in a cup?**
237 ml in a US cup, 250 ml in a metric cup, 284 ml in an imperial cup.

**How many teaspoons in a tablespoon?**
3 teaspoons in 1 tablespoon. 1 tablespoon = 15 ml, 1 teaspoon = 5 ml.

**What's the difference between a dry and liquid measuring cup?**
They measure the same volume, but liquid cups have a pour spout and space above the fill line to prevent spilling. Dry cups let you level off the top with a straight edge for precise measurement.

**Why do some recipes use weight instead of volume?**
Weight is more accurate. A cup of flour can vary from 115g to 155g depending on how you scoop it. Weighing guarantees consistency — which is why professional bakers always use grams.

**How do I convert a recipe from metric to US measurements?**
Multiply grams by 0.035 to get ounces. Divide millilitres by 237 to get cups. For small amounts, 15 ml = 1 tablespoon and 5 ml = 1 teaspoon.

**What's a stick of butter in grams?**
One US stick of butter = 113 grams (4 ounces). Two sticks = 227 grams (8 ounces / 1/2 pound).

**How much does 1 cup of water weigh?**
237 grams (8.34 ounces). Water's density makes volume-to-weight conversion straightforward — 1 ml of water = 1 gram.

**Can I use the same measuring cup for liquids and dry ingredients?**
You can, but it's less accurate. Liquid measuring cups are designed to be filled to the line and read at eye level. Dry measuring cups are meant to be filled and levelled. For best results, use each for its intended purpose.
