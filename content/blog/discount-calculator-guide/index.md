Every shopper has felt it — the excitement of a "50% off" sign, only to wonder later if you actually got a good deal. Discounts can be deceptive. A store can mark up the original price, stack confusing terms, or use psychological tricks to make you feel like you're saving more than you are.

This guide teaches you exactly how discounts work, how to calculate them in seconds, and how to spot the difference between a genuine deal and clever marketing.

## The Basic Discount Formula

The math behind any percentage discount is simple:

```
Sale Price = Original Price × (1 - Discount % / 100)
Savings = Original Price - Sale Price
```

### Example 1: Single Discount

A jacket priced at ₹2,500 with a 30% discount:

- Discount amount: ₹2,500 × 30% = ₹750
- **Sale price: ₹2,500 - ₹750 = ₹1,750**
- **You save: ₹750**

### Example 2: Multiple Discounts (Stacked)

Some stores offer discounts on top of discounts, like "20% off + 10% off." Important: these are **sequential**, not additive. A 20% + 10% discount is **not** 30% off.

Original price: ₹5,000

- After 20% off: ₹5,000 × 0.80 = ₹4,000
- After additional 10% off: ₹4,000 × 0.90 = ₹3,600
- **Final price: ₹3,600**
- **Total savings: ₹1,400 (28%, not 30%)**

The difference matters. On a ₹50,000 purchase, that 2% gap is ₹1,000.

## Types of Discounts

### Percentage Discount
The most common type — "X% off." Easy to calculate and compare across different price points.

| Discount % | ₹500 Item | ₹2,000 Item | ₹10,000 Item |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| 10% | ₹450 | ₹1,800 | ₹9,000 |
| 25% | ₹375 | ₹1,500 | ₹7,500 |
| 50% | ₹250 | ₹1,000 | ₹5,000 |
| 75% | ₹125 | ₹500 | ₹2,500 |

### Fixed Amount Discount
"₹500 off" — better for high-value items where a percentage might seem small.

- A ₹500 discount on a ₹2,000 item = **25% off** (excellent)
- A ₹500 discount on a ₹50,000 item = **1% off** (negligible)

The Rule: Fixed discounts favor cheap items. Percentage discounts favor expensive items. Know which applies to your purchase.

### Buy One Get One (BOGO)
BOGO deals are popular but their effective discount depends on how you calculate it.

| Deal Type | You Pay | You Get | Effective Discount |
|-----------|---------|---------|-------------------|
| BOGO Free | Price of 1 | 2 items | 50% |
| BOGO 50% Off | Price of 1.5 | 2 items | 25% |
| Buy 2 Get 1 Free | Price of 2 | 3 items | 33% |
| Buy 3 Get 1 Free | Price of 3 | 4 items | 25% |

A "Buy 1 Get 1 Free" sounds impressive but is mathematically equivalent to a **50% discount** on each item — not 100% off.

### Tiered Discounts
Common in bulk purchasing — the more you buy, the more you save.

| Quantity | Discount | Unit Price (₹100 item) |
|----------|----------|------------------------|
| 1-5 | 0% | ₹100 |
| 6-20 | 10% | ₹90 |
| 21-50 | 20% | ₹80 |
| 51+ | 30% | ₹70 |

Tiered discounts reward consolidation. If you know you'll need an item long-term, buying in bulk at the highest tier maximizes savings.

## How to Spot a Fake Discount

Not every "sale" is a real deal. Retailers use several tactics to make discounts look bigger than they are.

### 1. Inflated Original Price
The most common trick — a store raises the original price before applying a discount. A jacket that normally sells for ₹1,500 is listed at ₹2,500 with "40% off," bringing it to ₹1,500 — exactly the normal price.

**How to spot it:** Know the typical price of items you buy regularly. Use price tracker tools or check the price history on e-commerce sites.

### 2. Conditional Discounts
"Up to 50% off" — that "up to" is doing heavy lifting. Usually only a few outdated or unpopular items are at the maximum discount, while everything else is 5-15% off.

**How to spot it:** Ignore the headline percentage. Check the actual discount on the specific item you want.

### 3. Minimum Purchase Requirements
"20% off orders over ₹5,000" sounds great until you spend ₹5,100 to save ₹1,020 on items you didn't really need.

**How to spot it:** Calculate whether the minimum spend requirement pushes you to buy things outside your original shopping list. A 20% discount that makes you spend 50% more than planned isn't a saving.

### 4. Time Pressure
"Flash sale — ends in 2 hours!" creates artificial urgency to prevent you from comparing prices.

**How to spot it:** If it's a genuine flash sale on a known brand, the discount is probably real. If it's an unknown brand with a countdown timer, it's likely a permanent "sale" recycled regularly.

## The Real Price: Discounts + Taxes + Shipping

The discount applies to the item price, but your final cost includes other factors. Always calculate the **total out-of-pocket cost**, not just the discounted price.

### Example: Online Shopping

| Component | Without Discount | With 20% Discount |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Item Price | ₹3,000 | ₹2,400 |
| GST (18%) | ₹540 | ₹432 |
| Shipping | ₹200 | ₹200 |
| **Total** | **₹3,740** | **₹3,032** |

Your actual savings: ₹708, which is **18.9%** of the original total — less than the headline 20% due to fixed costs like shipping.

For cross-border shopping, include:
- Import duties and customs fees
- Currency conversion charges
- Return shipping costs (often not covered by discounts)

## Discount Stacking Strategy

When you have multiple discount opportunities, the order matters. Here's the optimal stacking order:

1. **Store-wide sale** (largest discount, usually applied first)
2. **Coupon code** (percentage off the already-reduced price)
3. **Cashback or store credit** (applied after all discounts)
4. **Loyalty points** (usually at the very end)

### Real Stacking Example

You're buying a ₹12,000 laptop during a store sale:

- Store sale: 15% off → ₹12,000 → ₹10,200
- Coupon code: 10% off → ₹10,200 → ₹9,180
- Cashback app: 5% back → ₹9,180 → ₹8,721 (cashback: ₹459)
- **Effective discount: 27.3%** (better than any single discount)

## Discount vs. Quality: When Cheap Costs More

A 70% discount on a ₹2,000 item sounds amazing until it breaks in three months and you have to buy another. Cost-per-use is a better metric.

| Item | Discounted Price | Lifespan | Cost Per Use |
|-----|-----------------|----------|--------------|
| Cheap shoes | ₹1,500 (50% off ₹3,000) | 6 months | ₹250/month |
| Quality shoes | ₹6,000 (no discount) | 3 years | ₹167/month |

The "expensive" shoes are literally cheaper per month. Apply this logic to:
- Electronics and appliances
- Furniture
- Work tools and equipment
- Clothing and footwear you use frequently

## FAQ

**What's the formula for calculating a discount?**
Sale Price = Original Price × (1 - Discount % / 100). For a ₹2,000 item at 25% off: ₹2,000 × 0.75 = ₹1,500.

**How do I calculate the discount percentage if I know the original and sale price?**
Discount % = ((Original - Sale) ÷ Original) × 100. If an item went from ₹800 to ₹600: ((200 ÷ 800) × 100 = 25% off.

**Is Buy 1 Get 1 Free the same as 50% off?**
Yes — they are mathematically identical. You get two items for the price of one, which is a 50% discount per item.

**How do I calculate a 20% discount quickly?**
Divide the original price by 10 (gives 10%), then multiply by 2. For ₹2,500: 10% = ₹250, 20% = ₹500 savings. Final price: ₹2,000.

**What's better: 25% off or ₹500 off?**
It depends on the price. For items under ₹2,000, ₹500 off is better (it's more than 25%). For items over ₹2,000, 25% off is better.

**Can I stack multiple discount coupons?**
Some stores allow it, most don't. Always read the terms — many coupons say "cannot be combined with other offers." When they can be stacked, they apply sequentially, not cumulatively.

**How do seasonal sales compare to regular discounts?**
Seasonal sales (Black Friday, End of Season, Diwali, Great Indian Sale) often offer genuine discounts of 40-70% on inventory clearance. However, some retailers inflate prices weeks before. Track prices beforehand to know the real deal.

**Should I buy something just because it's on sale?**
No. A discount on something you don't need is not a saving — it's spending. The best deal is 100% off something you never buy. Ask yourself: "Would I buy this at full price?" If the answer is no, the discount doesn't make it a good purchase.
