Whether you're following a recipe from a foreign cookbook, checking a weather forecast abroad, or working in a lab, temperature conversions come up constantly. Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin each serve different purposes — and switching between them is easier than you think.

This guide covers all three scales with formulas, mental shortcuts, reference tables, and practical examples you'll actually use.

## The Three Temperature Scales

| Scale | Abbreviation | Used Where |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Celsius | °C | Most countries worldwide (scientific + everyday) |
| Fahrenheit | °F | US, Bahamas, Cayman Islands, Liberia |
| Kelvin | K | Science, physics, astronomy, engineering |

## Celsius to Fahrenheit: The Most Common Conversion

### The Formula

```
°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
°C = (°F - 32) × 5/9
```

### Simple Mental Math

For quick estimates without a calculator:

- **Celsius to Fahrenheit:** Double it and add 30
  - 20°C → 20 × 2 + 30 = 70°F (actual: 68°F — close enough)
  - 30°C → 30 × 2 + 30 = 90°F (actual: 86°F)
  - 0°C → 0 × 2 + 30 = 30°F (actual: 32°F)

- **Fahrenheit to Celsius:** Subtract 30, then halve it
  - 80°F → (80 - 30) ÷ 2 = 25°C (actual: 26.7°C)
  - 100°F → (100 - 30) ÷ 2 = 35°C (actual: 37.8°C)

### Exact Conversion Trick

For precise conversion, memorise a few anchor points and interpolate:

- **0°C = 32°F** (water freezes)
- **16°C = 61°F** (mild day)
- **28°C = 82°F** (hot day)
- **37°C = 98.6°F** (body temperature)
- **100°C = 212°F** (water boils)

Every 5°C change equals roughly 9°F change.

## Kelvin: The Scientist's Scale

Kelvin is the SI base unit for temperature. It starts at absolute zero (-273.15°C) — the point where all molecular motion stops.

### Why Kelvin?

Kelvin uses the same step size as Celsius (1 K = 1°C), but with no negative numbers. This makes it essential for:
- Physics calculations (ideal gas law, thermodynamics)
- Astronomy (star temperatures)
- Engineering (cryogenics, materials science)

### Conversion Formulas

```
K = °C + 273.15
°C = K - 273.15
K = (°F + 459.67) × 5/9
°F = K × 9/5 - 459.67
```

## Practical Scenarios

### Scenario 1: You're Following an American Recipe

The recipe says "preheat oven to 350°F." Your oven is in Celsius.

350°F → (350 - 32) × 5/9 = **177°C** — set it to 175°C or Gas Mark 4.

### Scenario 2: Checking a Weather Forecast

You're travelling to New York and the forecast says 85°F. Is that jacket weather?

85°F → (85 - 30) ÷ 2 = **27°C** (approximate) — no jacket needed, it's T-shirt weather.

### Scenario 3: Science Class

You need room temperature in Kelvin for a gas law calculation.

22°C → 22 + 273.15 = **295.15 K**

### Scenario 4: A Child Has a Fever

Your digital thermometer reads 38.5°C. What's that in Fahrenheit?

38.5°C → 38.5 × 9/5 + 32 = **101.3°F** — yes, that's a fever.

## FAQ

**What's the easiest way to convert Celsius to Fahrenheit in my head?**
Double the Celsius and add 30. For 20°C: 20 × 2 + 30 = 70°F. It's approximate (off by ~2°F) but fine for weather and general use.

**Why does the US still use Fahrenheit?**
Historical reasons. Fahrenheit was the standard when thermometers became common, and the US never made the switch to metric. It's deeply embedded in American infrastructure, weather reporting, and cooking.

**At what temperature are Celsius and Fahrenheit the same?**
**-40°**. Both scales read the same at -40 degrees. This is the only point where they intersect.

**Do I need Kelvin for everyday use?**
No. Kelvin is primarily used in science, engineering, and astronomy. For cooking, weather, and general life, Celsius and Fahrenheit are sufficient.

**What's the highest possible temperature?**
Theoretically, the Planck temperature (1.416 × 10³² K) — beyond that, our current physics breaks down. For comparison, the core of the Sun is only 15.7 million K.

**Can water boil at a lower temperature?**
Yes. At higher altitudes, atmospheric pressure is lower, so water boils at a lower temperature. In Denver (1,600m elevation), water boils at about 95°C (203°F) instead of 100°C (212°F). This affects cooking times.

**What's absolute zero in Fahrenheit?**
Absolute zero is -459.67°F. It's the point where atoms stop moving entirely.

**How do I convert oven temperatures for fan-assisted vs conventional ovens?**
Fan ovens circulate hot air more efficiently, so you typically reduce the temperature by 20°C (or about 35°F). If a recipe says 200°C conventional, set a fan oven to 180°C.
