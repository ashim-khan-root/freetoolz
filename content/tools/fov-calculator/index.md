## FAQ

### What sensor size should I select?
Check your camera's specifications — the sensor size is usually listed as 1/3", 1/2.7", 1/2.5", or 1/1.8". If you're unsure, 1/3" is the most common for security cameras and a safe default. Larger sensors (1/1.8") capture more light but may have a different FOV at the same focal length.

### How does focal length affect what I see?
Shorter focal lengths (2.8mm) give a wider, panoramic view but less detail at a distance. Longer focal lengths (6mm, 12mm) zoom in on a narrower area but capture more detail of what's in that area. For general surveillance, 2.8-4mm is common; for identification purposes, 6mm or higher is better.

### What's the difference between optical zoom and digital zoom?
Optical zoom changes the focal length physically using lens elements — it captures more detail. Digital zoom simply crops and enlarges the image, reducing quality. This calculator is for fixed-focal-length lenses and optical zoom settings. Digital zoom does not change the FOV.

### How many cameras do I need for full coverage?
Divide the total area by the coverage width at your target distance, then add 20-30% overlap between cameras to avoid blind spots. For example, if each camera covers 40 ft wide and you need to cover 100 ft, plan for 3 cameras with overlap.

### Can I use this for PTZ cameras?
Yes — for PTZ (pan-tilt-zoom) cameras, calculate for the zoomed-out (widest) position to see the full coverage area, then calculate for the zoomed-in position to see the identification zone. This helps you position the camera so both extremes are useful.
