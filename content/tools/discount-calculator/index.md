## FAQ

### Is a 50% discount on a $100 item better than a 25% discount on a $200 item?
With discounts, the percentage matters but so does the original price. 50% off $100 = $50 saving and $50 final price. 25% off $200 = $50 saving but $150 final price. Same savings, different final spend. Use the calculator to compare apples to apples.

### How do I calculate the final price after multiple discounts?
For stacked discounts (e.g., 20% off + an extra 10% off), apply the first discount to get the first sale price, then apply the second discount to that reduced price. Do not simply add the percentages (20+10=30%) — that overestimates the discount.

### What's the difference between discount percentage and savings?
Discount percentage is the rate applied to the original price. Savings is the actual money amount you save. For example, 10% off a $500 item saves you $50, while 10% off a $50 item saves you only $5 — same percentage, very different savings.

### Can I use this for tax calculations?
Not directly. This calculator handles discounts only. For the final out-the-door price, calculate the discount first, then add tax to the discounted price separately.

### How do chain discounts work?
Chain discounts (like "20% + 10% + 5%") are common in wholesale. Apply them sequentially: 20% off original → then 10% off the result → then 5% off that result. The total discount is NOT 35% — it's less, since each discount applies to a smaller base.
