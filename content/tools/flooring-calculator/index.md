## How to Use the Flooring Calculator

1. **Choose the room shape** — select rectangle/square or L-shaped for rooms with an alcove or offset
2. **Enter room dimensions** — length and width for rectangles; each segment's dimensions for L-shaped rooms
3. **Set waste percentage** — 5-10% for straight/parallel installation, 15% for diagonal or patterned layouts
4. **Enter box coverage (optional)** — if flooring comes in boxes, enter how many sq ft per box to see exact box count
5. **Switch units** — toggle between feet and meters depending on your measurement system
6. **Read the results** — total area, material needed including waste, and number of boxes required

Perfect for homeowners, contractors, and DIYers installing hardwood, laminate, vinyl, tile, or carpet. All calculations happen in your browser.

## FAQ

### Why do I need to add waste percentage?
No room is perfectly square, and planks or tiles need to be cut at edges, around corners, and at doorways. Waste accounts for these cuts plus any mistakes. Straight installations need 5-10%, diagonal patterns need 15%, and rooms with many corners or obstructions may need more.

### What's the difference between hardwood and laminate calculation?
The area calculation is the same for any flooring type. The difference is in the waste factor (hardwood plank lengths vary more) and the box coverage (laminate comes in boxes, hardwood by the carton, carpet by the roll). The box calculator works for any material sold in fixed coverage units.

### How do I measure an L-shaped room?
Measure the first rectangle (the main area), then measure the second rectangle (the alcove or extension). Enter both sets of dimensions separately. The calculator adds them together and applies the waste percentage to the total.

### Can I calculate flooring for multiple rooms at once?
The calculator handles one room at a time. For multiple rooms, calculate each room separately and add the totals. This also lets you apply different waste percentages for different rooms (e.g., 5% for a simple rectangle bedroom vs 15% for a diagonal hallway).

### Should I buy all flooring at once?
Yes — buy all the material at once to ensure the same dye lot or batch number. Flooring from different batches can have slight color or pattern variations. It's better to have extra material (most stores accept unopened returns) than to run short and find the lot is discontinued.
