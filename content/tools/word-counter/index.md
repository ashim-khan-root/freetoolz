## How to Use the Word Counter

1. **Type or paste text** — enter your content in the text area. You can type directly, paste from your clipboard using Ctrl+V / Cmd+V, or use the sample button to load example text.
2. **Watch live statistics** — as you type or paste, the counters update instantly. You see word count, character count (with and without spaces), sentence count, paragraph count, and estimated reading and speaking times.
3. **Review keyword analysis** — the tool identifies the most frequently used words in your text, showing you a ranked list with counts. This helps spot overused terms.
4. **Copy or clear** — click "Copy" to copy your text, or "Clear" to start fresh. Your statistics reset automatically when you clear the text.
5. **Track reading and speaking time** — use the time estimates to plan presentations, voiceovers, or podcast scripts that fit within time limits.

## FAQ

### How does the reading time estimate work?
The reading time divides total word count by 200-250 words per minute, depending on content complexity. Simple blog content averages 250 WPM, while technical or academic content averages 200 WPM. The estimator uses 238 WPM as a balanced default.

### Does the word counter count numbers and symbols?
Yes. Numbers, symbols, and punctuation are all included. Words are defined as any sequence of characters separated by spaces. A number like "1,234" counts as one word. Symbols like "&" and "$" are included in character counts.

### How accurate is the sentence counter?
The sentence counter uses punctuation-based detection (periods, exclamation marks, question marks) combined with abbreviation handling to avoid false positives from abbreviations like "Mr." or "e.g."

### Can I use this tool offline?
Yes. The word counter runs entirely in your browser with no server communication. Once the page has loaded, you can disconnect from the internet and it continues working normally.

### How are keywords determined?
The tool counts word frequency, filtering out common stop words (the, and, a, an, in, etc.). The remaining words are ranked by frequency. This highlights content themes and reveals overused words that may need replacement.

## Tips

- Use the keyword analysis to check your writing for repetitive language — if you've used the same word 10+ times in a 500-word article, consider synonyms
- For SEO, aim for 1,500-2,500 words for standard blog posts and check that your target keyword appears in your top 5 keyword results
- When writing scripts for video or podcast, check the speaking time estimate — a 5-minute segment should target approximately 650-800 words depending on your pace
